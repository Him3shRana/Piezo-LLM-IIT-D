Piezoelectric Property: Yes

Evidence: The crystal belongs to the non-centrosymmetric space group C2 (No. 5), which is compatible with piezoelectricity. Ryan et al. (2015) directly measured shear piezoelectricity in self-assembled Fmoc-FF nanofibrils using lateral piezoresponse force microscopy (LPFM). The effective shear piezoelectric coefficient d₁₅' was determined to be 1.7 ± 0.5 pm V⁻¹, significantly above the detection limit of the measurement system (0.002 ± 0.103 pm V⁻¹ on non-piezoelectric glass). The piezoelectric response is attributed to the noncentrosymmetric nature of the underlying antiparallel β-sheet topology of the nanofibrils.

Notes:

Organic self-assembling peptide crystal consisting of Fmoc-diphenylalanine with partial water of crystallisation (0.875 H₂O per molecule).
Non-centrosymmetric crystal structure (space group C2), compatible with piezoelectricity.
Crystal structure determined from crystals grown from an H₂O–acetone gel phase at 100 K using synchrotron radiation (λ = 0.6889 Å) at Diamond Light Source (beamline I19).
The asymmetric unit contains two independent Fmoc-FF molecules, one fully occupied and one partially occupied water molecule (Z' = 2).
The R factor (R₁ = 10.27% for observed reflections) is relatively high due to the very small crystal size (0.070 × 0.009 × 0.008 mm³) and the inherent disorder/flexibility of the peptide.
Fmoc-FF self-assembles into nanofibrils via molecular stacking facilitated by the Fmoc moiety and mediated by noncovalent interactions including π-π stacking, hydrogen bonds, and van der Waals forces.
The molecular structure adopts a β-sheet conformation with a high degree of π-π stacking, forming antiparallel β-sheets that roll into cylindrical nanofibrils (~3 nm individual, ~66 nm in bundles).
Shear piezoelectricity was measured using lateral PFM; for comparison, FF nanotubes gave d₁₅' = 33.7 ± 0.7 pm V⁻¹ under identical conditions.
The piezoelectric response arises from net polarisation along the fibril axis due to the noncentrosymmetric arrangement of β-strands.
Fmoc-FF hydrogels are biomimetic materials with potential applications as piezoelectric scaffolds for tissue engineering, bone remodelling, and nerve regeneration.
